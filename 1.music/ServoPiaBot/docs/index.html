Project Story

The Vision

Traditional auto-playing devices strike keys with uniform force — soulless. Mechanical vibration and noise make the performance muddy. Hardcoded rhythms sound stiff and unnatural. ServoPiaBot asks a deeper question —

🧠

Learned Expression

Can a machine learn to feel? By varying servo angles and speeds, it produces uneven key velocities that mimic human touch and dynamics.

🎼

Musical Technique

Through fine-grained angle curves and velocity profiles, explore vibrato, glissando, and dynamic gradients — techniques only real performers master.

🤖

Autonomous Improvisation

The ultimate goal: let the machine learn to improvise on its own. Not just "play back" MIDI — truly perform music with creative expression.

🔧

Fully Open Source

Hardware, firmware, and host scripts — all open. From STM32 HAL libraries to the MIDI-to-C Python converter, everything is free to modify and share.

Design Evolution

From Concept to Reality

Four approaches evaluated, iterated, and narrowed down to the most practical solution.

Plan 4 · Rejected

Robotic Arm + Dexterous Hand

Immature technology, prohibitive DIY barrier, uncontrolled costs. And it will never match a human concert pianist anyway.

Plan 3 · Rejected

Slide Rail + Gripper

Cannot play complex chords; rail speed too slow for fast passages. Cost exceeds ¥1,000.

Plan 2 · Rejected

Push-Pull Solenoids

Loud physical noise. Individual solenoids (AH-520B, ¥7.5 each) cost more than servos. No mature open-source reference available.

Plan 1 · Final Design ✓

Digital Servo Array (PCA9685 + SG90 × 60)

SG90 servos at ¥5.5 each — 60 units for just ¥385. PCA9685 modules drive 16 PWM channels each; 4 daisy-chained modules handle all 64 servos. Mechanical noise can be mitigated via physical isolation and louder speaker volume. Mature open-source ecosystem (reference: automatic_gita).

Wiring

Wiring & Communication

I²C daisy-chain architecture — 4 PCA9685A modules controlling 60+ servos.

🔗 I²C Daisy-Chain Topology

  • STM32 I²C → PCA9685A #1 (0x40) → Servos 1–16
  • PCA9685A #1 → PCA9685A #2 (0x41, solder A0) → Servos 17–32
  • PCA9685A #2 → PCA9685A #3 (0x42, solder A1) → Servos 33–48
  • PCA9685A #3 → PCA9685A #4 (0x43, solder A0+A1) → Servos 49–60

⚡ Power Architecture

  • LRS-150-5 → PCA9685A V+ / GND (servo power)
  • ST-Link → STM32 (debugging power)
  • Standalone: 2S Li-Po battery or power bank for STM32

⚠️ Safety

Safety Warnings

⚡

Measure Before Wiring

Always test the 3-core power cable with a multimeter (AC 750V range). L–N = 220V, L–E = 220V, N–E = 0V before use. Some vendors mislabel live and neutral wires!

🧤

Never Work on Live Circuits

Unplug before wiring. Wear insulated gloves. 220V AC is lethal — treat it with absolute respect.

🔌

Separate Power Domains

Servo power and MCU power must be isolated. LRS-150-5 dedicated to the servo array; STM32 powered via ST-Link or battery independently.

🔊

Noise Mitigation

60 servos running simultaneously produce noticeable mechanical noise. Mitigate with physical isolation, vacuum soundproofing, and increased speaker volume.
